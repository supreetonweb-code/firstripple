First Ripple

A project to build the institution Indian finance has always needed.

Every year, India's finest business schools produce thousands of finance graduates. They arrive at their first desk fluent in theory, sharp in examination, and almost entirely unprepared for the work that will actually be asked of them.

The gap is not one of intelligence. It is one of design. Finance education in India was built to produce graduates who could pass — not practitioners who could lead. The curriculum has not kept pace with what markets demand, what technology has changed, or what employers quietly stopped expecting from campus hires.

Something was always going to have to change. The question was who would do it, and whether they would do it right.

Tier 1 Business School will be a finance institution built from the demand side backward. Not from a syllabus outward. Employers will co-own the curriculum. Every faculty member will have lived the work they teach. Every graduate will have made real decisions before they are asked to make them professionally.

The first institution designed around what finance actually demands — not what academia has always assumed it does.

It will not be another program. It will be a different kind of institution — one that takes seriously the question of what a Day 1-ready finance professional actually looks like, and builds everything in service of that answer.

Before the institution has a campus, before its first student has enrolled, before the world knows its name — there is this. A project. A gathering of the people who will make it real. First Ripple is the quiet beginning of something that will move outward for decades.

A ripple does not announce itself. It does not ask permission. It begins at a single point — precise, deliberate — and travels. What we build here, in these early months, will determine the shape of everything that follows.

The institution is being built by Supreet Singh — who spent two decades inside Indian finance and BFSI, watching the gap between education and employment widen, and who decided that watching was no longer enough.

This work is dedicated to Abhay Aima — who saw it first, and who was meant to build it alongside us.
