The Institution That Doesn't Exist Yet

India is the world's fifth-largest economy with one of Asia's premier financial centres at its heart. In the next twenty years, it will not merely participate in global finance — it will define it. And yet there is no institution in this country that the world points to and says: that is where India's best finance talent comes from. That gap is remarkable. Given what India has become. Given what India is going to become.

Every BFSI business leader has lived this quietly. Talented graduates, selected through every right process, from the best campuses — and yet most needed a year before they were genuinely useful. Not from lack of intelligence. They lacked the clarity of someone built for a direction — a specialist worthy and ready for the seat, not a generalist still finding their way. You wished their institutions had done more with the two years they had. The unease was real, even if it went unsaid.

Beneath that is a deeper problem. The mid-senior layer of any serious BFSI organisation — the people it actually depends on — is thin in the pipeline behind it. Great finance leaders have always been accidents of mentorship, fortune, and timing. Never the product of deliberate formation. And now artificial intelligence is compressing the timeline for everyone, changing what work means, what skills matter, who organisations will need to lead them through what is coming.

India's great management institutions were built to produce managers who could move across industries. BFSI was one industry among many for them. Today it is an entire world — with its own language, its own physics, its own professional culture. The curriculum built around theory and recall was never designed for the complexity of a financial market in 2025. No one failed. The world moved faster than institutions can.

This is a design failure — and design failures are not fixed by incremental improvement. They require building something new.

NYU Stern, US · 1900 · FT #23
New York was becoming the financial capital of the world and needed a school that understood finance as its native language.
CEIBS, China · 1994 · FT #8
Western MBAs were arriving in China and failing. The country needed management education built for its market complexity.
ISB, India · 2001 · FT #27
India's finest future managers were going abroad to be trained and eventually lost. The country needed a world-class school.

Each of the above were born from the same conviction: something important needed to exist to solve a particular problem, no other institution was dedicated to solving it, and the moment to build it had arrived. The white space in India today is finance. The institution that combines domain depth, practitioner-led learning, and an AI-native architecture to produce the leaders the next era demands does not yet exist. It is being built in Mumbai, co-owned and co-designed with the industry it serves.

In 2045, the fingerprints of one institution will be quietly woven through the generation running India's most consequential financial institutions.

That institution is being built today.
